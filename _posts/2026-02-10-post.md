 Manakongtreecheep, K, Ctortecka, C, et al. developed a genetically encoded peptide library system, termed Pepyrus, that enables high-throughput identification of rare HLA-bound peptides. They designed an oligo pool in which the peptide library is conjugated to a Strep affinity-tagged Smt3, a yeast small ubiquitin-related modifier (SUMO) protein, to facilitate high-throughput SUMO–peptide fusion protein purification via a Ulp1 cleavage site. The constructs were expressed in E. coli, and the peptide can be extracted from the bacteria. 
 
<img src="/assets/images/Peprus.png">

The authors demonstrated that the Peprus-produced peptide library enables improved peptide identification, especially when combined with data-independent acquisition (DIA) Mass Spectrometry (MS), leading to high recovery of tumour-associated antigen (TAA), neoantigens, and endogenous retrovirus-derived peptides from cancer cells. 

Such a system offers the following advantage over the chemical synthetic peptide library:
1. High-throughput generation of user-defined peptides in a cost-effective way.
2. The off-the-shelf library is reusable for high-confidence immunopeptidome characterisation of multiple patient samples.
3. Allows identification of rare neoantigens.

<h3>Questions to be addressed</h3>
1. What are the potential sources of biases in LC-MS methods which hamper the high-complexity Pepyrus libraries?
2. How to differentiate near-identical peptide sequences that may not be distinguished using current techniques? (i.e. single amino acid difference of leucine and isoleucine)
3. How can Peprus be applied to study autoimmune diseases and infectious diseases?
